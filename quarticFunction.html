Math Problem: Quartic Function & Triangle Area

Problem: A quartic function has two minima at $(-a, 0)$ and $(a, 0)$. The turning points form an equilateral triangle. The area under the curve is 1 unit greater than the area of the triangle. Find $a$.

Step-by-Step Derivation

1. Establish the Equation

Since the curve touches the x-axis at $-a$ and $a$, these are repeated roots. The equation is:

$$y = k(x^2 - a^2)^2$$

2. Use Equilateral Triangle Properties

The vertices are $(-a,0)$, $(a,0)$, and the maximum at $(0, ka^4)$.

Base length $= 2a$. Height of equilateral triangle $= 2a \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = a\sqrt{3}$.

Equating the function height to the triangle height:

$$ka^4 = a\sqrt{3} \implies k = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{a^3}$$

3. Calculate Areas

Triangle Area: $A_{tri} = \frac{1}{2}(2a)(a\sqrt{3}) = \sqrt{3}a^2$

Curve Area: Integrating $\int_{-a}^{a} y dx$ yields $A_{curve} = \frac{16\sqrt{3}}{15}a^2$

4. Solve for $a$

We are given $A_{curve} - A_{tri} = 1$:

$$\frac{16\sqrt{3}}{15}a^2 - \sqrt{3}a^2 = 1$$ $$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{15}a^2 = 1 \implies a^2 = 5\sqrt{3}$$ $$a = \sqrt{5\sqrt{3}}$$
Final Answer:
a ≈ 2.943
