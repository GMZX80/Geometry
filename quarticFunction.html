The Mystery of the Quartic Curve

The Goal: We need to find the value of $a$. We are given a W-shaped curve and an equilateral triangle hidden inside the turning points. We know the area under the curve is exactly 1 unit larger than the area of the triangle.

1. Visualizing the Problem

This interactive graph shows the actual solution. The blue area is the integral, and the red dots are the "Tent" (the triangle).

2. Step-by-Step Solution

Step 1: Finding the Equation (The "Bouncing Ball")

Analogy: When a graph touches the x-axis and turns back around (like a ball bouncing), it creates a squared factor in the equation.

Because the curve "bounces" at $x = -a$ and $x = a$, our equation is:

$$y = k(x + a)^2(x - a)^2$$

We simplify this using the difference of two squares:

$$y = k(x^2 - a^2)^2$$

Note: $k$ is the "stretch factor"—it decides how tall the curve is.

Step 2: Building the Tent (Geometry)

The turning points form an Equilateral Triangle.

  • The Base: The distance from $-a$ to $a$ is $2a$.
  • The Height: For an equilateral triangle with side $2a$, the height is $a\sqrt{3}$.
The Connection: The height of the triangle is the same as the height of the curve at $x=0$. $$ \text{Height} = k(0^2 - a^2)^2 = ka^4 $$ So: $ka^4 = a\sqrt{3} \implies k = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{a^3}$

Step 3: Calculating Areas (Calculus)

Now we compare the sizes of the "Tent" (Triangle) and the "Hill" (Curve).

Area of Triangle:

$$A_{tri} = \frac{1}{2}(\text{base})(\text{height}) = \frac{1}{2}(2a)(a\sqrt{3}) = \sqrt{3}a^2$$

Area under Curve:

We integrate the function from $-a$ to $a$. (Or 0 to $a$ and double it):

$$A_{curve} = 2 \int_{0}^{a} k(x^2 - a^2)^2 \,dx = \frac{16k a^5}{15}$$

Substitute our $k$ value from Step 2:

$$A_{curve} = \frac{16}{15} \left( \frac{\sqrt{3}}{a^3} \right) a^5 = \frac{16\sqrt{3}}{15}a^2$$

Step 4: The Final Showdown

The problem says the curve area is 1 unit bigger than the triangle area.

$$ A_{curve} - A_{tri} = 1 $$ $$ \frac{16\sqrt{3}}{15}a^2 - 1\sqrt{3}a^2 = 1 $$

Think of $\sqrt{3}a^2$ as an object. We have $\frac{16}{15}$ of them minus $1$ (which is $\frac{15}{15}$). That leaves $\frac{1}{15}$.

$$ \frac{1}{15}\sqrt{3}a^2 = 1 $$ $$ \sqrt{3}a^2 = 15 \implies a^2 = \frac{15}{\sqrt{3}} = 5\sqrt{3} $$
Final Answer: a ≈ 2.943
