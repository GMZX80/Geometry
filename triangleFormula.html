Deriving the Inradius of an Isosceles Triangle (Red Triangle)

We are going to find a formula for the inradius of an isosceles triangle where:

  • the two equal sides meet at the top with angle \(A\) (the apex angle),
  • the base is at the bottom,
  • the area of the triangle is \(\mathcal A\).

We will build this formula from scratch, using only:

  • the area formula \(\text{Area} = \frac12 ab \sin C\), and
  • the inradius relation \(r = \dfrac{\text{Area}}{s}\), where \(s\) is the semiperimeter.

Think of this as a small “story”: we start from side lengths and angles, slowly link them to the perimeter and area, and finally reach the inradius.

Step 1 – Name the sides and the angle

Take an isosceles triangle and give names to the important parts:

  • Let each of the two equal sides have length \(b\).
  • Let the base (the bottom side) have length \(c\).
  • The angle between the two equal sides at the top is the apex angle \(A\).

So the picture in your head is: two equal sides \(b\) forming an angle \(A\) at the top, and a base \(c\) along the bottom.

Step 2 – Express the area in terms of \(b\) and \(A\)

We now use the standard formula for the area of a triangle when we know two sides and the angle between them:

\[ \text{Area} = \frac12 ab \sin C. \]

In our triangle:

  • both sides are \(b\),
  • the included angle is \(A\).

So the area \(\mathcal A\) of this triangle is

\[ \mathcal A = \frac12 b \cdot b \sin A = \frac12 b^2 \sin A. \]

This equation links the side length \(b\) to the area and angle. Rearranging it, we can write \(b^2\) in terms of \(\mathcal A\) and \(A\):

\[ b^2 = \frac{2\mathcal A}{\sin A}. \]

We will substitute this later so that \(b\) disappears from our final formula.

Step 3 – Relate the base and semiperimeter to \(b\) and \(A\)

Next we need the semiperimeter \(s\), because the inradius is \(r = \dfrac{\text{Area}}{s}\). To find \(s\), we first need the base length \(c\).

3a. Find the base using a right triangle

Drop a perpendicular from the apex down to the base. In an isosceles triangle this perpendicular does two helpful things:

  1. It bisects the base, so each half is \(c/2\).
  2. It bisects the apex angle, so each of the two top angles becomes \(A/2\).

Now focus on one of the right-angled triangles created. In that triangle:

  • the hypotenuse is \(b\),
  • the angle at the top is \(A/2\),
  • the side opposite this angle is \(c/2\).

Using \(\sin(\theta) = \dfrac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}\), we get

\[ \sin\frac{A}{2} = \frac{c/2}{b}. \]

Rearrange to make \(c\) the subject:

\[ c = 2b\sin\frac{A}{2}. \]

3b. Find the semiperimeter \(s\)

The full perimeter \(P\) of the triangle is

\[ P = b + b + c = 2b + c. \]

Substituting our expression for \(c\),

\[ P = 2b + 2b\sin\frac{A}{2} = 2b\left(1 + \sin\frac{A}{2}\right). \]

The semiperimeter \(s\) is half the perimeter:

\[ s = \frac{P}{2} = b\left(1 + \sin\frac{A}{2}\right). \]

Now we have \(s\) expressed neatly in terms of \(b\) and \(A\).

Step 4 – Use the inradius formula

For any triangle, the inradius \(r\) (radius of the incircle) is given by

\[ r = \frac{\text{Area}}{s}, \]

where \(s\) is the semiperimeter. In our triangle,

\[ r_1 = \frac{\mathcal A}{s} = \frac{\mathcal A}{\,b\left(1 + \sin\frac{A}{2}\right)}. \]

We now square both sides to get an expression for \(r_1^2\):

\[ r_1^2 = \frac{\mathcal A^2}{\,b^2\left(1 + \sin\frac{A}{2}\right)^2}. \]

This formula almost does what we want, but it still contains \(b^2\). We will now remove \(b^2\) using the area relation from Step 2.

Step 5 – Eliminate \(b\) using the area expression

From Step 2 we had

\[ b^2 = \frac{2\mathcal A}{\sin A}. \]

Substitute this into the expression for \(r_1^2\):

\[ r_1^2 = \frac{\mathcal A^2} {\left(\frac{2\mathcal A}{\sin A}\right) \left(1 + \sin\frac{A}{2}\right)^2}. \]

It can be easier to see what’s happening if we treat it as a product:

\[ r_1^2 = \mathcal A^2 \cdot \frac{\sin A}{2\mathcal A} \cdot \frac{1}{\left(1 + \sin\frac{A}{2}\right)^2}. \]

Now one factor of \(\mathcal A\) cancels:

\[ r_1^2 = \frac{\mathcal A \sin A} {2\left(1 + \sin\frac{A}{2}\right)^2}. \]

This is the general formula for the square of the inradius of our isosceles triangle in terms of its area \(\mathcal A\) and apex angle \(A\).

Step 6 – (Specific problem) Replace \(\mathcal A\) by \(A\)

In the original competition question, the triangle was chosen so that its area is numerically equal to the angle \(A\) (in square units versus degrees). That is,

\[ \mathcal A = A. \]

If we now make that substitution, we get the special-case formula used in the solution:

\[ r_1^2 = \frac{A \sin A} {2\left(1 + \sin\frac{A}{2}\right)^2}. \]

But the important part of this page is the journey: starting from side lengths, finding area and semiperimeter, using the inradius formula, and carefully eliminating \(b\) step by step until only \(A\) and \(\mathcal A\) remain.
