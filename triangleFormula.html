Deriving the Inradius Formula for the Red Triangle

We want to show that for an isosceles triangle with

  • apex angle \(A\),
  • area \(\mathcal A\),
  • inradius \(r_1\),

we have

\[ r_1^2 = \frac{\mathcal A \,\sin A}{2\left(1 + \sin\frac{A}{2}\right)^2}. \]

Only at the very end will we use the special condition of the question, \(\mathcal A = A\).

1. Set up the triangle

Consider an isosceles triangle with

  • two equal sides of length \(b\),
  • apex angle \(A\) between those two sides,
  • base of length \(c\).

So the two equal sides meet at the top, forming the angle \(A\), and the side at the bottom is the base \(c\).

2. Area in terms of \(b\) and \(A\)

For any triangle with sides \(a, b\) and included angle \(C\), the area is

\[ \text{Area} = \frac12 ab \sin C. \]

Here both sides are \(b\) and the included angle is \(A\), so

\[ \mathcal A = \frac12 b \cdot b \sin A = \frac12 b^2 \sin A. \]

Rearrange to express \(b^2\) in terms of \(\mathcal A\) and \(A\):

\[ b^2 = \frac{2\mathcal A}{\sin A}. \]

3. Base length and semiperimeter

Drop a perpendicular from the apex to the base. In an isosceles triangle, this:

  • bisects the base,
  • bisects the apex angle \(A\) into two angles of \(A/2\).

This gives two congruent right-angled triangles. In one of them:

  • the hypotenuse is \(b\),
  • the angle at the apex is \(A/2\),
  • half of the base is the opposite side: \(b \sin\left(\dfrac{A}{2}\right)\).

So the full base length is

\[ c = 2b \sin\frac{A}{2}. \]

The perimeter is \(2b + c\), so the semiperimeter \(s\) is

\[ s = \frac{2b + c}{2} = \frac{2b + 2b\sin\frac{A}{2}}{2} = b + b\sin\frac{A}{2} = b\left(1 + \sin\frac{A}{2}\right). \]

4. Use the inradius formula \(r = \dfrac{\text{Area}}{s}\)

For any triangle, the inradius \(r\) satisfies

\[ r = \frac{\text{Area}}{s}, \]

where \(s\) is the semiperimeter. So here

\[ r_1 = \frac{\mathcal A}{s} = \frac{\mathcal A}{\,b\left(1 + \sin\frac{A}{2}\right)}. \]

Now square both sides:

\[ r_1^2 = \frac{\mathcal A^2}{\,b^2\left(1 + \sin\frac{A}{2}\right)^2}. \]

Substitute the expression for \(b^2\) from step 2:

\[ b^2 = \frac{2\mathcal A}{\sin A}. \]

Then

\[ r_1^2 = \frac{\mathcal A^2} {\left(\frac{2\mathcal A}{\sin A}\right) \left(1 + \sin\frac{A}{2}\right)^2}. \]

Rewrite this as a product to simplify:

\[ r_1^2 = \mathcal A^2 \cdot \frac{\sin A}{2\mathcal A} \cdot \frac{1}{\left(1 + \sin\frac{A}{2}\right)^2}. \]

Cancel one \(\mathcal A\):

\[ r_1^2 = \frac{\mathcal A \sin A} {2\left(1 + \sin\frac{A}{2}\right)^2}. \]

This is the general formula they quote.

5. Apply the special condition \(\mathcal A = A\)

In the specific contest problem, the triangle is chosen so that its area is numerically equal to the angle \(A\):

\[ \mathcal A = A. \]

So we simply replace \(\mathcal A\) with \(A\) in the formula:

\[ r_1^2 = \frac{A \sin A} {2\left(1 + \sin\frac{A}{2}\right)^2}. \]
